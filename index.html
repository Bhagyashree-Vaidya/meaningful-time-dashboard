Are Americans losing meaningful time?

We studied 200,000+ daily diaries over 22 years. The answer surprised us: earning more money doesn't help, but spending time with people does.

Finding 1
The Money Question

More money does not mean a more meaningful life

People across all income levels rate their days almost the same on meaning and happiness.

How meaningful does each income group feel?

We split people into 4 income groups. The scores are nearly identical. Meaning scoreHow meaningful people feel their daily activities are, on a scale of 0 to 10 barely changes across income.
What this means

Whether you earn $25K or $250K, your day feels about equally meaningful.

Why it matters

Policies focused only on income growth miss what actually makes people's lives feel worthwhile.

Key insight

The things that make life feel meaningful are available to everyone, regardless of income.

In simple terms

A teacher and a CEO can have equally meaningful Tuesdays.

What about the difference between meaning and happiness?

Meaning and happiness are not the same thing. Childcare feels deeply meaningful (+0.90 gap) but is often stressful. Watching TV feels pleasant but scores low on meaning (-1.80 gap). This matters: if we only measure happiness, we miss what gives life purpose.

Finding 2
The Connection Question

Everything feels more meaningful when done with other people

Every single activity we measured scores higher when you do it with someone else.

Same activity, different experience

The social premiumHow much more meaningful an activity feels when done with others instead of alone is the gap between the two bars. Bigger gap = bigger boost from company.
What this means

A meal alone scores 7.1. The same meal with someone? 8.2. That's a 15% boost just from company.

Why it matters

Workplaces, schools, and neighborhoods that help people connect aren't luxuries. They're well-being infrastructure.

Key insight

The single easiest way to make any activity more meaningful: do it with someone.

In simple terms

Even boring chores feel better when you have company.

Psychological Safety as Infrastructure

Paid work alone scores 6.75. With colleagues: 7.36. Yet 86% of the decline in meaningful time traces to rising solitude. Creating spaces where people feel safe to connect isn't a soft perk. It's the most cost-effective well-being investment available.

Finding 3
The Trend

Americans are spending more time alone, and meaning is falling

Over 22 years, how meaningful people feel about their day has slowly declined. Loneliness is the main driver.

How meaningful does the average day feel? (2003-2024)

The meaning scoreAverage rating of how meaningful daily activities feel, on a 0-10 scale, across all Americans has dropped steadily. The affect scoreHow pleasant or happy people feel during activities, on a 0-10 scale. This measures mood, not purpose. (happiness) barely moved.
What this means

Americans feel about the same level of happiness as 20 years ago, but their days feel less purposeful.

Key insight

We're maintaining happiness but losing meaning. That's a problem conventional metrics miss entirely.

Time spent with other people (2003-2024)

The alone time sharePercentage of waking hours spent without any other person present jumped sharply around 2020 and hasn't recovered.
What this means

In 2010, people spent about 39% of their day alone. By 2024, it's 47%. That's an extra ~1.2 hours alone every day.

Why it matters

This isn't just a pandemic effect. The trend started before COVID and hasn't reversed.

What's driving the decline? (Full breakdown)

We broke down the -0.114 point decline over 21 years into its causes:

The biggest culprit: time spent alone is becoming less meaningful (-0.070 points). People also shifted time from high-meaning activities (socializing, education) toward low-meaning ones (TV, streaming).

Finding 4
The Opportunity

Community, inclusion, and care are the highest-return investments

The activities with the highest meaning scores are about connection and care, not consumption.

Which activities are gaining and losing time?

Positive values mean an activity helped offset the meaning decline. Negative values mean it made the decline worse.
What this means

Americans lost 11 min/day of social time (score: 8.55) and gained 6.5 min/day of passive screen time (score: 6.20). That swap alone accounts for a huge share of the decline.

Key insight

Shifting just 10 minutes from TV to social time would meaningfully reverse the national trend.

Policy Implications

Three evidence-based levers: (1) Community spaces that make social participation easy and accessible; (2) Care economy investment to support the highest-meaning activities (childcare: 9.01, socializing: 8.55); (3) Workplace belonging that closes the gap between solo work (6.75) and connected work (7.36). These aren't soft amenities. They are measurable, high-return well-being infrastructure.

Who is losing the most meaningful time?

Demographic shifts between 2010 and 2024:

Fewer parents (41% to 35%) cost -0.007 meaning points. Aging population (avg 32.5 to 38.7) cost -0.006. More education (+9.2 years) helped slightly (+0.004). The takeaway: inclusive programs that bring different ages, family types, and backgrounds together can offset these structural shifts.

If you remember one thing

Money can't buy meaningful time, but spending time with people can. And America is doing less of it every year.
